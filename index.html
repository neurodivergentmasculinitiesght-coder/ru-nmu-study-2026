What this study is about

This is a Masters research study in medical anthropology. It explores how neurodivergent male students experience daily life at university: how they navigate relationships, expectations around being male, whether they feel like they belong, and how having or not having a formal diagnosis shapes their access to support.

The study does not approach neurodivergence as a problem to be fixed. It approaches it as a real and varied human experience that universities often do not understand or accommodate well enough. The goal is to understand that experience from the inside, through the stories of the people living it.

This research treats your experience as knowledge. You are not a subject. You are a contributor to a study that aims to make universities better for people like you.

Three questions drive the study: how masculinity is performed or reworked among neurodivergent male students, what institutional practices shape their sense of belonging or exclusion, and how the presence or absence of a formal diagnosis shapes identity and access to support.

Who can take part

🧠

You identify as neurodivergent

This includes ADHD, autism, dyslexia, dyspraxia, dyscalculia, Tourette syndrome, sensory processing differences, or any other neurological difference. The list is not exhaustive.

🎓

You are an NMU student

Currently enrolled at Nelson Mandela University. Undergraduate or postgraduate. Any year, any faculty. No restrictions on your course of study.

👤

You identify as male

This study uses a broad understanding of what it means to identify as male. It is not tied to gender assigned at birth. Any student who identifies as male is welcome.

A formal clinical diagnosis is not required. Self-identification is accepted and treated as equally valid. Requiring a diagnosis would exclude many people whose neurodivergence has simply never been assessed, which is common in South Africa.

Students who have been hospitalised for a mental health related reason within the past six months or who are currently receiving crisis intervention or acute psychiatric care are not eligible to participate at this time. Being in regular therapy or receiving routine psychological support is not an exclusion criterion.

What participation involves

Participation is flexible. There is no single method everyone must follow. You engage in the way that works best for you. Below is what the study uses and what each involves.

💬

Semi-structured interviews

A conversation about your experiences. Between 60 and 90 minutes. You can receive the questions in advance. You choose where we meet. You can pause or stop at any time.

👁️

Participant observation

The researcher is present in campus spaces over the fieldwork period. This is not surveillance. It is observing how the university environment works in everyday moments.

📸

Photovoice (optional)

You take photos of your daily university life using your own phone and discuss them with the researcher. Completely optional and entirely participant-led.

🗣️

Informal conversations

Casual conversations that happen naturally during observation. Not recorded. The researcher writes notes afterwards. Nothing is attributed to you by name.

How it works, step by step

1

You reach out

Send a message to the research email address. Just introduce yourself. No formal application. No paperwork yet.

2

You receive the information sheet

A plain-language document explaining exactly what participation involves, what your rights are, and how your data will be protected. Take as long as you need to read it.

3

You ask any questions you have

There is no pressure to decide immediately. The researcher will answer any questions before you make any decision.

4

You consent on your own terms

Consent can be written, verbal, or in another format that works for you. Interview questions can be sent to you in advance. You choose where and how we meet.

5

You participate in the way that works for you

At least one interview. Other methods are additional and flexible. You can withdraw from any part of the study at any point without giving a reason.

6

You receive the findings

Once the study is complete, you will be offered a plain-language summary of the key findings and kept informed of any publications or presentations.

Your rights as a participant

  • Participation is entirely voluntary. You will never be pressured to join or to stay.
  • You can withdraw at any point without giving a reason. This has no effect on your academic standing, NSFAS funding, or relationship with any support service at NMU.
  • Your real name will never appear in any research output. Pseudonyms are used consistently across all materials.
  • Everything you share is confidential and used for research purposes only. It will not be shared with NMU staff, disability services personnel, or any other institutional representative.
  • If you engage with photovoice, you retain full control over which images are used. Consent for image use is confirmed again at the point of write-up.
  • Transport and data costs that arise directly from your participation will be covered by the researcher.
  • If anything comes up during the study that is emotionally difficult, you will be provided with information about the Student Counselling and Career Centre at NMU. Support is always available.
  • You can request access to your own data at any point and ask for it to be withdrawn and destroyed.
  • All data is stored in compliance with the Protection of Personal Information Act No. 4 of 2013 and retained for five years after the study concludes.
